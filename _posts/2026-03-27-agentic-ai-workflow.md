After attending ASPLOS, one thing became hard to ignore: everybody seems to be talking about agentic AI.

That feeling was both exciting and uncomfortable. For a long time, my own use of large language models was fairly lightweight. I mostly used web-based tools in the familiar way: ask a question, get an answer, iterate a little, then move on. Only very recently did I start using a more coding-centered setup with VS Code and Codex. Compared with people who already seem fluent in agentic workflows, tool use, automation, and multi-step AI-assisted development, I felt behind.

I do not think that feeling is unique to me. It is easy to look around at a conference and feel that everyone else has already learned the new language of a field while you are still trying to understand the basic vocabulary. In this case, the vocabulary includes ideas like agent loops, tool calling, planning, delegation, verification, and building systems where the model does more than just respond to a prompt.

What struck me at ASPLOS was that agentic AI is no longer being discussed as a novelty. People are starting to treat it as an expected way of working. The conversation is shifting from "can this help?" to "how should we structure our workflow around it?" That difference matters. It means the challenge is not only learning how to use a model well, but learning how to design a process around the model.

For me, that realization created some urgency. If I only stay at the level of using web interfaces for isolated questions, I am probably missing the larger change. I need to understand how people build agentic workflows, why those workflows help, where they break down, and how to use them in a way that is actually productive rather than performative.

So this is the mindset I want to adopt going forward: instead of feeling embarrassed about being late, I want to treat this as a chance to learn carefully from the beginning. I want to understand the stack from the ground up. What does a useful agent actually do? When should it plan first? When should it call tools? How should it keep context? How should it check its own work? What parts should stay human-driven?

I am also realizing that learning agentic AI is not just about learning a new tool. It is about learning a new workflow. That means building intuitions about decomposition, feedback loops, trust, and verification. It also means understanding the difference between a flashy demo and a workflow that genuinely saves time in research or engineering practice.

Right now, I am still early in that process. But I think the right response to feeling behind is not panic. It is curiosity plus deliberate practice. My goal is simple: understand enough to build workflows that are useful, reliable, and aligned with the way I actually work.

This post is the starting point.
